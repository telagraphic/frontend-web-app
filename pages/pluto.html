Pluto

Pluto, once considered the ninth planet of our Solar System, is now classified as a dwarf planet and the largest known object in the Kuiper Belt. Discovered in 1930 by Clyde Tombaugh, Pluto was reclassified as a dwarf planet in 2006 by the International Astronomical Union. This distant world, named after the Roman god of the underworld, orbits the Sun at an average distance of 3.7 billion miles (5.9 billion kilometers) and completes one orbit every 248 Earth years.

Astronomy

Pluto is a dwarf planet with a diameter of about 1,473 miles (2,377 kilometers), making it smaller than Earth's Moon. Its surface is covered with frozen nitrogen, methane, and carbon monoxide, creating a complex and varied landscape. Pluto has a thin atmosphere that expands when it's closer to the Sun and collapses when it's farther away. The dwarf planet has a highly elliptical orbit that sometimes brings it closer to the Sun than Neptune, though their orbits never actually cross due to their different orbital planes.

Pluto's Moons

Pluto has five known moons, with Charon being the largest and most significant. Charon is so large relative to Pluto that some astronomers consider Pluto-Charon to be a binary system. The other four moons - Nix, Hydra, Kerberos, and Styx - are much smaller and were discovered between 2005 and 2012 using the Hubble Space Telescope.
