Uranus

Uranus, the seventh planet from the Sun, is an ice giant and the third-largest planet in our Solar System. Named after the Greek god of the sky, Uranus was the first planet discovered with a telescope, found by William Herschel in 1781. This distant world has a distinctive pale blue-green appearance caused by methane in its atmosphere. Located approximately 1.8 billion miles (2.9 billion kilometers) from Earth, Uranus completes one orbit around the Sun every 84 Earth years, and its unique feature is that it rotates on its side, with its axis tilted at nearly 98 degrees.

Astrology

In astrology, Uranus represents innovation, rebellion, sudden change, and awakening. Known as the "Planet of Revolution and Freedom," Uranus governs our desire for independence, originality, and breaking free from conventional thinking. It's associated with the zodiac sign Aquarius, embodying qualities of humanitarianism, progressiveness, and the ability to think outside the box. Uranus represents our capacity for sudden insights, technological advancement, and the revolutionary changes that lead to personal and collective evolution.

Alchemy

Within alchemical traditions, Uranus symbolizes the principle of sublimation and the element of Air in its most revolutionary and transformative state. It represents the process of sudden elevation and awakening, where consciousness breaks free from established patterns and rises to new levels of awareness. Uranus embodies the principle of transformation through sudden insight and represents the alchemical stage where consciousness becomes liberated and capable of perceiving reality from entirely new perspectives, transcending conventional limitations.

Astronomy

Uranus is an ice giant with a diameter of about 31,763 miles (51,118 kilometers), making it the third-largest planet in our solar system. Its atmosphere is composed primarily of hydrogen (83%) and helium (15%), with about 2% methane that gives it its distinctive blue-green color. Uranus's most unique feature is its extreme axial tilt of 97.77 degrees, meaning it essentially rotates on its side. This unusual orientation causes extreme seasonal variations, with each pole experiencing 42 years of continuous sunlight followed by 42 years of darkness.

Uranus's Moons

Uranus has 27 confirmed moons, all named after characters from the works of William Shakespeare and Alexander Pope. The five largest moons - Miranda, Ariel, Umbriel, Titania, and Oberon - were discovered between 1787 and 1948. Miranda, the smallest of the major moons, has one of the most varied and complex surfaces in the solar system, featuring canyons, ridges, and impact craters. The other moons are much smaller and were discovered by the Voyager 2 spacecraft in 1986 or by Earth-based telescopes in more recent years.
